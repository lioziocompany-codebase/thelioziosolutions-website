Delivery-based e-commerce has struggled to achieve meaningful penetration in Nigerian secondary cities. Here is the case for why a pickup-first model, when built correctly, is not a compromise; it is a competitive advantage.

The economics of last-mile delivery outside Lagos are punishing. Address systems are inconsistent, traffic is unpredictable, and the cost of a single failed delivery attempt can erase the margin on an entire order. Most delivery-based platforms respond by passing that cost on to the customer, pricing themselves out of the market they were meant to serve.

A pickup-first model removes that cost structure entirely, but only if the pickup experience itself is fast and reliable enough to earn the customer's trust. That is an operational discipline, not a shortcut: it means a designated pickup location, a warehouse operation built for speed, and a standard for how quickly an order is ready and handed over. When that discipline holds, pickup stops feeling like a compromise and starts feeling like the more dependable option.

We did not arrive at this model by assumption. We proved it, in Ibadan, before we said a word about scaling it anywhere else. That is the same discipline behind every decision we make: prove before you scale, measure before you move. Pickup-first is not where we started because it was easy. It is where we landed because it was correct.
