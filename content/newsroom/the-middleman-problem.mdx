Every layer between a manufacturer and a Nigerian consumer adds cost. Remove enough of those layers, and you can offer the customer a better product at a lower price while earning a better margin. Here is the arithmetic that drives the LIOZIO sourcing model.

In a fragmented supply chain, a product typically passes through several hands before it reaches a household — each one taking a markup to cover its own overhead, and each one adding time before the product reaches the shelf. None of those layers add quality. They add cost, and in a market where the consumer is watching every naira, cost is the thing that decides whether a household buys again.

Drumroll's sub-brands — Goodfood, Bountiful, and Joombow — are built to source directly from manufacturers and producers instead. That removes several of those markup layers in one step, and the saving does not disappear into margin alone; a meaningful share of it is passed on to the customer, which is what makes repeat purchase possible in a price-sensitive market.

This is not a one-time pricing trick. Direct sourcing only works as a durable advantage if the operational discipline behind it — supplier relationships, quality standards, inventory accuracy — holds at scale. That discipline is what we are proving in Ibadan before we take the same sourcing model to any other city. The arithmetic only works if the operation behind it is real.
